## Definition

If $f:[a,b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ is [continuous](/notes/areas/mathematics/real_analysis/definitions/continuity/) on $[a,b]$ and [differentiable](/notes/areas/mathematics/real_analysis/definitions/differentiable/) on $(a,b)$, then there exists a point $c \in (a,b)$ where



$$
f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}
$$



## Proof

Define the auxiliary function



$$
g(x) = f(x) - \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}(x - a)
$$



Then $g(a) = f(a)$ and $g(b) = f(a)$, so $g(a) = g(b)$.

By Rolle's Theorem (a special case of MVT where $f(a) = f(b)$), there exists $c \in (a,b)$ where $g'(c) = 0$.

Computing $g'(c) = f'(c) - \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} = 0$ gives the result.
