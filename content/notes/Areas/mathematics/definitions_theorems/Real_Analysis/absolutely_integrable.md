A function $f$ is **absolutely integrable** on a domain $D$ if



$$
\int_D |f| < \infty
$$



Equivalently, $f \in L^1(D)$.

## One Dimension



$$
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |f(x)| \, dx < \infty
$$



## Two Dimensions



$$
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |f(x,y)| \, dx \, dy < \infty
$$



## Relation to Conditional Integrability

A function may be **conditionally integrable** (the integral $\int f$ converges) without being absolutely integrable. For example:



$$
\int_1^\infty \frac{\sin x}{x} \, dx \quad \text{converges, but} \quad \int_1^\infty \left|\frac{\sin x}{x}\right| dx = \infty
$$



Absolute integrability implies conditional integrability, but not conversely.

## Significance

Absolute integrability is a sufficient condition for the existence of the [Fourier transform](/notes/areas/mathematics/definitions_theorems/functional_analysis/fourier_transform/).
